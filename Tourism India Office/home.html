Valley Civilization

The Indus Valley Civilization, also known as the Harappan Civilization, flourished around 3300 BCE along the banks of the Indus River. It was a remarkable urban civilization renowned for its sophisticated city planning, advanced drainage systems, and intricate craftsmanship.

With major settlements like Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa, this civilization showcased remarkable trade, artistry in pottery, and an early form of writing. Its decline around 1300 BCE remains a mystery.

Masala Dosa

The Masala Dosa stands as an iconic South Indian delicacy. It's a thin, crispy pancake crafted from a fermented batter of rice and urad dal lentils, creating its unique texture.

The filling often comprises a spiced potato mixture known as "aloo masala," infused with turmeric, green chilies, mustard seeds, curry leaves, among other spices.

Typically served with coconut and tamarind chutneys along with sambar, a lentil and vegetable stew, it offers a delightful blend of flavors and textures.

Butter Chicken

Butter Chicken, a North Indian classic, is renowned for its velvety texture and delectable fusion of flavors. Chicken pieces marinated in a spice blend of yogurt, lemon juice, and spices are grilled or seared before being simmered in a rich, creamy sauce crafted from tomatoes, butter, cream, and spices like garam masala, ginger, and garlic.

Appreciated for its smoothness, balanced sweet-spicy profile, and creamy texture, Butter Chicken is often served with basmati rice or naan, a traditional flatbread, perfect for soaking up the sauce.

HERITAGE

Generations carry the legacy of our cultural heritage

India is a treasure trove of heritage and culture and is one of the few countries in the world where the past remains inextricably linked to the present. The wealth of heritage spanning the ages, in terms of personalities, monuments, architecture, arts & crafts, history and culture cannot be measured

Holi - Color Festival

Holi, known as the Festival of Colors, is a vibrant Hindu celebration marking the arrival of spring.

Lasting for a day and a night, it's characterized by joyous gatherings where people throw colored powders and water at each other. This jubilant occasion signifies the triumph of good over evil and the end of winter's gloom, fostering a sense of unity and merriment

Families and friends come together to dance, sing, and exchange sweets and delicacies. It's a time when social barriers are broken, and people of all backgrounds join in the festivities, spreading love and happiness.

Diwali - Festival

Diwali is a major festival of India celebrating the victory of good over evil. The meaning of the word Diwali is ‘lighted lamps’ so it is rightfully called the festival of lights. This festival is celebrated by lighting small ‘diyas’ and candles and burning fireworks and crackers.

All this was done to celebrate the returning of Lord Ram to Ayodhya after fourteen years of exile. In present times all cities in India are brightly lit with dazzling lights with Diwali parties being organized for a fortnight before the main festival day.

Diwali is as big a festival in India as Christmas for the West. The festive celebration continues for days with families getting together exchanging gifts. Prayers and rituals mark the festive spirit with children enjoying the special sweets and fireworks
